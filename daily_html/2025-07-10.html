Targeting Melanoma-Specific Tyrosinase: Cyclic Peptide Disrupts Actin Dynamics for Precision Apoptosis Induction

Melanoma is an aggressive and highly metastatic cancer that exhibits stubborn resistance to conventional therapies, highlighting the need for novel treatments. Existing therapeutic strategies often suffer from systemic toxicity, poor efficacy and fast-gained drug resistance. In this study, we designed a cyclic peptide system (c-RGDKYQ) that takes the advantage of the overexpression of tyrosinase in melanoma cells to trigger enzyme-mediated oxidation and self-assembly. The assembled peptide nanostructures can selectively disrupt the actin cytoskeleton, impairing cancer cellular functions, e.g., motility, adhesion, and proliferation, ultimately leading to apoptosis. This approach does not rely on external drug payloads or complex delivery mechanisms. c-RGDKYQ exhibits high selectivity for melanoma cells, strongly suppressing tumor growth in a murine model with minimal systemic toxicity. Our findings illuminate that, through targeting tyrosinase, c-RGDKYQ may be an enzyme-responsive alternative to conventional treatments for melanoma.

TLDR: This paper presents a cyclic peptide (c-RGDKYQ) that targets melanoma by exploiting the overexpression of tyrosinase. Upon tyrosinase-mediated oxidation, the peptide self-assembles into nanostructures that disrupt the actin cytoskeleton, leading to apoptosis in melanoma cells both in vitro and in vivo with minimal systemic toxicity.

Explanation

The study designs a cyclic peptide that selectively targets melanoma cells. The peptide (c-RGDKYQ) self-assembles into nanostructures upon oxidation by tyrosinase, an enzyme overexpressed in melanoma. These nanostructures disrupt the actin cytoskeleton, impairing cell motility, adhesion, and proliferation, ultimately inducing apoptosis. The research includes in vitro studies demonstrating selective cytotoxicity and disrupted actin filaments, as well as in vivo studies in a murine model showing significant tumor growth suppression with minimal toxicity. This approach avoids external drug payloads or complex delivery mechanisms.

Open, Reproducible Calculation of Assembly Indices

We present assembly-theory, a Rust package for computing assembly indices of covalently bonded molecular structures. This is a key complexity measure of assembly theory, a recent theoretical framework quantifying selection across diverse systems, most importantly chemistry. assembly-theory is designed for researchers and practitioners alike, providing (i) extensible, high-performance implementations of assembly index calculation algorithms, (ii) comprehensive benchmarks against which current and future algorithmic improvements can be tested, and (iii) Python bindings and RDKit-compatible data loaders to support integration with existing computational pipelines.

TLDR: The paper introduces 'assembly-theory', a Rust package for calculating assembly indices of covalently bonded molecules, offering a high-performance, extensible, and reproducible tool with Python bindings, designed to quantify selection in chemical systems. It addresses the computational challenges in assembly theory with cross platform support, memory safety and convenient parallelism.

Explanation

This paper presents a software package, 'assembly-theory', implemented in Rust, for calculating assembly indices. Assembly theory is a framework useful in quantifying selection in chemical systems. The authors emphasize the package's high performance, extensibility, and reproducibility. Key features include implementation of assembly index calculation algorithms, comprehensive benchmarks, and Python/RDKit integration. The paper addresses the need for efficient and accessible tools in this domain, given that existing solutions may be approximate, closed-source, platform-dependent, or written in less common languages. The tests and benchmarks performed show that the Rust implementation outperforms other implementations (Go).
